REVIEW ARTICLE

# Beyond the Pale: Reviewing the Relationship between Material Culture and Design History

Production of Culture/Cultures of Production

PAUL DU GAY (ed.). Sage, 1997. 248 pp., b&w illus. £14.00 paper. ISBN 0 7619 5436 8.

American Material Culture: The Shape of the Field

ANN SMART MARTIN and J. RITCHIEGARRIsON (eds.). University of Tennes-see Press, 1997. 428 pp., b&w illus.\$39.95 cloth. ISBN 0 912724 358.

Popular Culture and Everyday Life TOBY MILLER and ALEC MCHOUL. Sage, 1998. 224 pp., b&w illus. £14.99 paper. ISBN 0 7619 5212 8.

Border Fetishisms: Material Objects in Unstable Spaces

PATRICIA SPYER (ed.). Routledge, 1998. 262 pp., 8 b&w illus. £12.99 paper. ISBN 0 415 91857 X.

Even though fashion in scholarship may not change as conspicuously nor at such a fast rate as in the garment industry, it nevertheless operates just as evidently in the changing trends of academic research. In the last twenty years consumption studies has grown from a precocious intruder to become an essential ingredient in the understanding of the history of design.1 The most recent apparent shift in methodological approaches, pushing the boundaries of design history even further from the production/consumption debate, has been to acknowledge the importance of material culture as a more inclusive umbrella under which it is possible to include the social life of things beyond their commodity phase.² The interdisciplinary implications of a material culture approach in the study of the history of design that does not exclude any artefact or part of the mundane everyday object-world meant venturing into territories that were once considered beyond the pale. Investigating the history of design primarily for what it can reveal about the social meaning of things, rather from the more usual judgemental functional or aesthetic standpoint, is risky either way—dangerous for those who still see themselves as guardians of 'good design', and unpredictable for those venturing into unknown territory without a 'good design' compass.

It is necessary to define 'material culture' in view of the liberal way the term has been used to refer to a number of very different, although often inter-related types of objects and issues, ranging from empirical object-based analyses in the decorative arts, to contextual museum studies involving the politics of display, and to the branch of social anthropology concerned with the symbolic meaning of artefacts, to name just three.³ Even more generally it is also associated by implication with disciplines, on the one hand, like anthropology which considers culture as determining social action, and, on the other, several generations of sociology-derived cultural studies that have critiqued the materialist theory of Marxism which asserts that 'the mode of production of material life determines the general character of the social, political and spiritual processes of life.' The excuse for this crude over-generalization is to point out the relation between the decline in confidence in deterministic production models for the interpretation of the history of design with the increase in interest in consumption and material culture as a less rigid framework for the investigation of the relationship between designed objects and those persons and institutions who conceive, produce, sell, buy and use them.

The most remarkable characteristic of material culture studies, and one that in a way it shares with design history, is its hybridity. Both fields emerged from the need for a new kind of investigative approach that could take into account the new products, practices and spaces of modernity that could not readily adapt to the regimes of their parent disciplines—anthropology and art history. Design history has attempted to demarcate its territory quite specifically, although not altogether successfully, as testified by the recurring debates concerning what objects of study should be included within its remit. Meanwhile, material culture studies, at least as represented by the Journal of Material Culture, refuses to give its allegiance to any one particular discipline, and manages to consort with varying degrees of success with a broad range of cognate fields within the arts and sciences, reflecting the broad usage of the term.5 History, archaeology, sociology and cultural studies, anthropology, geography, museology, literary, religious and psychoanalytical studies, are just some of the areas that have focused on something called 'material culture' as a particular way of engaging with their disciplines through the vehicle of the material world of things. The literature available on material culture is growing at such a phenomenal rate that even experts like Daniel Miller, in a recent survey of new books on the field, admitted that he has 'abandoned any attempt to retain an overview of the field'.6 Yet because it is still a relatively recent fledged area, even among those who actually practise material culture, not all are aware of its scope. In his essay, 'Material culture history: the scholarship nobody knows', Cary Carson from Colonial Williamsburg Foundation asks: 'Why have material culture studies produced so much excellent scholarship that nobody knows and nobody heeds?'"

Material culture' is quite a familiar term to design historians, increasingly adopted as an interchangeable alternative to 'design history'. Both areas also share the cross-disciplinary practice of drawing from an eclectic mix of sources and resources. However, it has to be said that design history has been much more pragmatic in its agenda, mainly seeing its brief to train design historians to serve the design profession through design education, museum services, and the so-called cultural industries as journalists, media researchers and producers, public and private sector gallery professionals. Although, as mentioned above, there is some acknowledgement of consumption as a cultural practice in the history of design, there is no clear agenda on broader issues beyond the profession of investigating the role of design within the context of sociality. It is the ambitious scope of an intellectual project that engages directly with contemporary cultural production missing in twentieth-century design history studies that can be found in some of the cross-disciplinary endeavours in material culture studies. Current redefinitions of design history that encompass theory as part of a more generic area of 'Design Studies'8 seem to be taking into account the need for the contemporary dimension to make the field relevant to the discipline of design at present. But attempts to draw such tightly defined boundaries only seem to threaten to enclose it even further into an academic backwater. An interdisciplinary approach recognizing the shared interests between material culture and design history/theory (studies) would seem to offer a mutually compatible means of engaging with, and contributing much more directly to, the wider theoretical debates of cultural production across the humanities as well as the social sciences.

The books selected for this review are intended to give a representative view of four quite specific and contrasting types of literature that are directly and indirectly associated with current trends in material culture studies and 'the everyday' and to show what they have to offer the design historian in particular. Although I started out by mentioning 'fashion', it must be clear by now that I do not regard the changes and shifts of focus in design history as cosmetic, but as symptomatic reflections of deep social and cultural changes inscribed in the material world and, therefore, as evident in the history of design. It can no longer be possible to take as given what constitutes appropriate design theories, objects or practices to apply in the study of the history of design if it is to be relevant to contemporary concerns.

Beyond the specialized discipline of design history, and for all the efforts that it has exercised to dispel the stereotypical narrow view of the 'useful' or 'cult' object, etc., nevertheless the assumption persists that its subject matter is mainly limited to modern design classics and the output of professional designers since the industrial revolution, together with the perception of an agreed methodology of investigation based on researching 'the facts'. A typical example of such an approach found in a students' handbook on design history still in print until last year was John Heskett's dismissal of 'anonymous' design as inadequately researched design history.

My characterization of design history above, of course, bears little relation to the actual research that has been carried out in its name since the

197os when it broke away from art history to form its own identity as a distinct discipline.1º Nor does it take into account the many and varied approaches that have been used to study the history of design. But relatively little of the more interesting research has found its way into print, a fact borne out by the range and extent of actual publications available." What sustains the stereotype that design history's main concerns continue to be the ideal object, the style of the decade, century, etc. and the designer as hero, is much more to do with publishers' conservative insistence on the clichéd view of design as classified under the 'visual arts'"and ultimately, even more pragmatically, on which shelf the bookseller places 'design history' titles. It is extremely difficult to subvert the pervasiveness of a classification system that lends design history a particular identity by locating it alongside books on the 'decorative arts' or 'antiques' as if it belonged there naturally. Given its 'good design' pedigree, it is therefore not suprising that the bulk of the literature that is available should concern itself with matters of so-called 'poor' design and critiques of consumerism derived from a watered-down version of commodity fetishism that has little resonance with contemporary scholarship, or for that matter with practising designers' interests.12

Within the disciplinary specialism, however, where there is no simple agreement over design history's remit or practice, it remains a recurring subject of debate. Richard Buchanan's recent review of Jonathan Woodham's Twentieth-Century Design cited 'Giedeon's "epochal" history', Forty's "exemplary" history' and 'Heskett's "disciplinary"history' as examples of the variety of approaches that have made contributions to the study of the history of design.13 But it castigates Woodham for his greater emphasis, as if such a view were a radical departure, on 'the role and behaviour of the consumer' and asks 'Does the author seek to reorient design history within the framework of cultural studies, focused on material culture?'15 The criticism would appear to be provoked by the centrality that Woodham, according to Buchanan, confers upon the consumer, and the fear that such an emphasis somehow suggests a 'dumbing down' of standards encouraging 'poor' design and thereby a slur on the work of professional designers. Significantly, both Buchanan and Woodham mention 'the everyday' in connection with 'material culture', hinting at some kind of acknowledgement of the 'popular' as a deviation from the 'normal' subject matter of design studies. The reference to 'the everyday’ as deviant is entirely dependent on what kind of design is being considered. As soon as one looks, for instance, at graphic design, or feminist critiques of design history, it is apparent that such an assumption becomes a travesty. Nor does it conform to all design historians' view of what constitutes appropriate subject matter for the study of the history of design.16 It is just this kind of joinedup interest and engagement with the popular, the fashion system, the consumer, the designer, and the cultural transformation of 'design' once it leaves the 'visual' arena to enter the accumulation of ordinary things that turns it into 'the everyday', that an interdisciplinary material culture approach can accommodate.

The selection of four books under review here represents two texts that talk about material culture as physical objects, and two that refer to the contexts within which culture is constructed rather than in the objects through which it is made physically manifest. Of the first pair, American Material Culture mainly refers to 'material culture' as the artefacts within the privileged space of the museum or the 'heritage' site, and Border Fetishisms privileges the object as a focus of primary attention and interest, deliberately concentrating on materiality and overturning the negative association of the fetish. In the second pair of texts, neither Production of Culture/Cultures of Production nor Popular Culture and Everyday Life refer to materiality. Nevertheless, the concepts and methods discussed in order to explain various aspects of contemporary culture depend on their realization in material form within the domain of the everyday. The kind of cultural analyses represented by these last two texts take place within the arena of the material world where cultural theory is tested out by the detailed investigation of the interaction between people and things. But they shy away from placing things at the centre of attention because they derive from the type of cultural studies that consider 'materialism' as synonymous with fetishism and therefore as tainted. However, although objects are not foregrounded, it doesn't take much searching to come across illustrative examples of objects such as Miller and McHoul's reference to 'punk's use of rubbish as adornment: bag liners, lavatory appliances and ripped and torn clothing.'7 And just opening a page of Du Gay at random finds an illustration of The interior of a United Colors of Benetton store'.18

American Material Culture: The Shape of the Field is an anthology of fourteen essays derived from a conference hosted by the Winterthur Museum in Delaware in 1993 with an agenda to map the current state of American material culture studies. It followed on from an earlier conference held in 1975 with a similar remit to investigate objects as a means of understanding and interpreting American culture. The multidisciplinarity of the project is reflected in the mix of academic and museum backgrounds of the authors. Each essay presents a case study with the common objective of researching a particular aspect of Americanness with varying degrees of focus on material culture as an interpretive method, questioning its appropriateness to the study of ethnic, national, gender and individual identity, and discussing various types of museum display and techniques. The case studies included: animal artefacts in nineteenth-century domestic interiors, twentieth-century dolls in an educational museum project, the intimate 'secret' meanings of portrait miniatures, the social dimension of Tupperware, leather stockings and hunting shirts as representative of

American frontier heroes, technical 'owners'manuals' that helped in the dissemination of the ideals of standardized production, the colonial significance of the front porch in American domestic architecture, a refutation of the 'melting pot' model of American ethnic assimilation through the study of archaeological remains, and so on. Apart from the fascination of the particular studies for their intrinsic interest and value, many offered insights into ways of looking which could be adapted to other studies, although the interpretation of the objects is primarily devoted to defining the particularities of American national identity. Some of the essays deal with 'variability' of identity and distinctive ethnicities as in the case of John McCarthy's essay 'Material Culture and the Performance of Sociocultural Identity', which makes a study of burial practices from the archaeological study of the First African Baptist Church Cemeteries in Philadelphia applying 'acculturation' in preference to the more traditional 'assimilation' model discussed by James Gregory Cusick in 'Archaeological Perspectives on Material Culture and Ethnicity'.

Anotherevident characteristic common to the essays in American Material Culture is the concern with the interpretation of the object as 'document' within 'multiple contexts' but with particular reference to its place within the context of the museum and the heritage site. In 'EvaluatingExhibitions: HistoryMuseums and Material Culture', Ellen Paul Kenker bases her essay on period rooms around the politics of museum practice and addresses basic questions about the audience and the application of scholarship to the display of objects as a means of interpreting the past. The dominant perspective of the anthology is from that of the museum curator interpreting objects for the benefit of the 'observant viewer', with an awareness of such practicalities as the impact of the baby-stroller on the pace of the museum visitor.19 This aspect is not the most original of the anthology since it has been amply dealt with elsewhere by British and American authors who have written extensively about collectors and collections from museological, anthropological and sociological points of view.20 Kenker's assertion that the 'crux of material culture study [is] to understand which object proves the clearest intersection with a cultural concept’ discloses the perspective of a museum curator using objects to convey a story rather than as a primary source in the study of sociocultural history. Keenly aware of competition posed by the entertainment industry, she speculates that 'there may be little reason for the public to go to a static museum display for their visit to "nostalgia land".' Significantly enough the Cooper-Hewitt NationalDesign Museum in New York recently mounted an exhibition on the Disney Theme Parks called 'The Architecture of Reassurance',21 possibly suggesting a certain amount of self-interested curiosity in researching the popularity of the nostalgic escape supplied by the popular Disney mythological interpretation of American history.

Apart from the variety supplied by the individual contributions, the value of the anthology lies in the way the amalgam of essays under one cover applies and illustrates the usefulness of various types of material culture analyses based on a range of methods, citing almost everybody from Henry Glassie² and James Deetz²3 to Ian Hodder²4 and Dick Hebdige.25 But in the end the effect is that of a unitary project tellingly expressed by Cary Carson of Colonial Williamsburg Foundation who confesses that 'material culture studies is still energized by our zeal for a populist American history .. . we still want to believe that this history of material life is the gateway to a more open, more equal, more democratic, more popular American History.'26 His view of material culture as down to earth—'you know— tables and chairs'—betrays a lack of regard for 'the academy' and theoretical approaches such as that of Christopher Tilley's groundbreaking Reading Material Culture,27 which, according to Carson, 'sell artefacts short'. This is not an opinion shared by all the authors and seems a rather unfair criticism particularly in view of the vast amount of literature which he ignores, such as that dealing with consumption directly related to material culture.28 Carson's surprisingly prepostmodern perspectivethat his 'professional responsibility is to contribute to the larger task of rewriting a unified narrative of the nation's past', might derive from the imperative of the heritage-driven museum. So although there is much to be learnt from the American branch of the material culture field it should be seen as only one part of a much larger and growing interdisciplinary field.

Patricia Spyer's Border Fetishisms: Material Objects in Unstable Spaces represents a very contrasting approach, focusing on the 'irreducible materiality' of objects as a way of coming to grips front-on with the distinctive meaningfulness of 'things'. The anthology sprang from a conference²º based on the notion of a 'border fetish'as a way of investigating the materiality of different types of cultural encounters across mind/matter borders and through the vehicle of the 'fetish' in which neither the object's materiality nor its historicity can be reduced. A common point of reference for all the authors is provided by William Pietz's genealogy of the fetish derived from West African colonial trade and anthropological studies of an object that managed to subvert the definition of capitalist exchange.30 In 'The Spirit of Matter' Peter Pels describes its eventual metaphoric adoption by two of the most important intellectual endeavours of Western modern discourse—Marxism and psychoanalysis, as the articulation of desire whether in sexuality or the over-valuation of Western commodities, in which 'the fetish remained an object of abnormal traffic'.31He argues with some of the most avowed material culturists for concentrating too much on matters of consumption rather than the matter itself, asserting that 'things can talk back' presenting fetishism as 'animism with a vengeance'.

Material culture studies concentrates on the process of meaning acquisition and locates the site of such transactions in the object itself and therefore where object/subject relations should be investigated. When the fetish object is cast in the role of identity formation, as is the case of Border Fetishisms, it presumes a special quality that makes its materiality more stubbornly percipient, more obvious and less amenable to cultural appropriation because it was specifically brought into existence to subvert any attempts to colonize, take over, reform or exchange it for something else. The feature that characterizes a fetish is that its function is always over-determined. Conventional design studies can only really deal with functional objects and their static symbolic representation. Border Fetishisms offers an alternative methodological approach for dealing with such questions.32

Speyer's anthology re-evaluates the negative connotations accrued by fetishism through a series of suggestive case studies that reveal the nonnegotiable character of things even in the case of artefacts like paper money specifically created for exchange.3 Whereas it may be easy to conceive of the fetish as a means of subverting the colonizing intent of cloth gifting 'to subjugate peoples in order to consecrate and give substance to colonial policies',ª4 Adela Pinch's case study of shoplifting is used as a means of 'raising questions about how a thing comes to be legitimately one's own'.35

The most stimulating essay of the collection—'Marx's Coat' by Peter Stallybrass—is remarkable in its brilliantly simple analysis of the negative misunderstanding that is commonly attributed to Marx's use of the concept of fetishism. Using Marx's own overcoat as an analogy he describes how when Marx was hard up and had to pawn his coat, transforming it into exchange value and thus entering the capitalist economic system, he could not go to the British Library and research Capital and had to resort to journalism to earn his living. Whereas when he was prosperous enough to afford to wear his coat he could present a respectable enough appearance to be admitted to the British Library, only to pawn it once again to buy paper. According to Stallybrass what Marx ridiculed in his critique of commodity fetishism, was not fetishism nor the object itself but the fetishization of the commodity—'the evacuated non-object that was the site of exchange', interpreting Capital as 'Marx's attempt to give back the coat to its owner'.3 Stallybrass reminds us that Marx used 'materialism'to refer to 'the animised material object of human labour and love' vividly exemplified in the nineteenth-century clothes repairers' term 'memories' used to refer to the wrinkles in the elbows of sleeves in worn garments.37

The last two titles Production of Culture/Cultures of Production and Popular Culture and Everyday Life do not deal with 'material' culture as such. They are both textbooks that present methods and theories with illustrative examples for the interpretation of cultural production concentrating on the processes of meaning construction. While not entirely ignoring the physical world, the main focus is on theoretical models and methods of analysis quite far removed from the object world. Production of Culture is one of a series of textbooks on cultural studies produced by the Open University, each of which addresses one aspect of what the editors refer to as 'the cycle of culture' comprising: representation, regulation, identity, consumption and production. Its main project is to look at the centrality of culture as productive, in direct line with the Frankfurt School of critical theory that originally set out to finesse Marxian analysis by refusing the deterministic model of earlier crude interpretations.38 The Sony Walkman is the subject of one of the six books in the series³ and is the only one that presents a case study of an actual object. It tracks the trajectory of the artefact through the various stages of the 'cycle', reminiscent of John A. Walker's earlier 'Production/Consumption'model⁴ºand Igor Kopytoff's 'cultural biography of things'.41 While in theory cultural studies may, as the editors claim, 'make sense of our everyday lives', it is difficult to conceptualize that process without bringing objects into the equation.

What Production of Culture does with its holistic approach is to locate design within a network of systemic convergences rather than to prioritize the economic over the cultural and thus separate them into discrete compartments. It introduces the concept of discourse as the explanatory means through which the relationship between 'culture' and 'economy' is mutually constitutive. In this schema 'design' is acknowledged to have a 'pivotal'role as a 'cultural intermediary' in the signifying practices of advertising and production of a multitude of goods and services—a phenomenon that has not gone unnoticed by design historians. The Open University's textbook house-style of providing a highly structured model and a standardized set of pre-defined theoretical terms, while excellent of its kind, nevertheless might irritate those sensitive to 'jargon'and the authorial command-style voice-over form of address. There is an undoubted advantage to the set of common reference points provided, both within each textbook and cross-referenced to the other volumes in the series, making it an ideal teaching tool with built-in aims and objectives, activity suggestions, bibliographies and readings. At the same time, it locks the reader into a particular holistic theoretical worldview which gives little space for independent thought. And while the readings are selected to give a variety of points of view, they are ultimately used to illustrate an interpretation that conforms to a self-referential 'circuit of culture' framework to which the different authors contribute on a united front. This is not the place to go into the fors and againsts of the principle of using a set of textbooks as a pedagogic tool. Although it makes it easy for the tutor and the student to have a total picture of the mechanism of 'culture' between the covers of one set of six books, it can nevertheless lead to a false sense of security where there is little left to question or doubt.

Fashion is the most elusive of all the cultural processes and an area that has traditionally been trivialized by academics but is now increasingly recognized as a significant aspect of consumption. Chapter 3 of Culture of Production, 'Fashion: Unpacking a Cultural Production', states that 'fashion' is 'often used as if it exclusively refers to apparel'. It instances business management, law and medicine as some of the 'wide variety of fields' where it also operates but where 'the idea that they are involved with fashion would be derided'. This would agree with my opening remarks to this review—that fashion can equally apply to changes in academic practices. It is therefore disappointing to find that, rather than uncovering one of the less obvious workings of the fashion system, it should fix on the most clichéd and conventional—that of fashion clothes—to illustrate the culture of fashion, thereby seeming to reinforce the notion that it is not a serious area of study. It is also here that Cultures of Production is particularly unconvincing in its attempt to pin down the caprices of fashion to the 'circuit' formula, baldly stating that The essence of fashion in clothes is that it compels us to discard a garment before it has outlived its usefulness.'42 What it fails to mention is that it is often not even bought for its usefulness in the first place. And even when consumers can be shown to conform to 'conspicuous consumption' or some other equally classic critique of consumerism, that does not necessarily explain the working of the culture of fashion except to reinforce the concept of it as trivial or redundant, etc. A material culture approach such as those in Border Fetishisms, that depends on a more ethnographic approach, soon shows how particular cases on the ground constantly confound any attempts to be locked into a theoretical system⁴³ by bringing us back to the physical object.

I would not want to conclude that there is not much to commend Production of Culture when it is used selectively. In a way, if it were to be posited as a case study of its own theory it would be possible to view it as a standardized cultural product that has swallowed its own theory about the way that culture is produced. What is missing from the schema is precisely the facet that a material culture perspective would provide by taking it beyond its own internal system. It would investigate how readers, teachers, students and others use the textbooks, what they take from it and how they apply it, what they reject, how they adapt and interpret the material, in spite of the seamlessly constructed pedagogical paradigm presented by its authors. But the messiness of how to do such research, what to do with it and ultimately how to interpret findings that are difficult to quantify and therefore to synthesize into generalizations that cannot easily be refuted would not fit the neatness of such a totalizing theory. So much so that one could be permitted to speculate that the case studies of firms like Benetton and Sony have been selected because they prove the theory rather than produce the theory. The only book in the series that is not governed by the 'circuit' theory because it starts from the inves tigation of a particular product, comes closest to a material culture study. In Doing Cultural Studies: The Story of the Sony Walkman⁴4 theory is employed as an investigative tool to give ‘a dynamic picture of how commodities are given meaning through their insertion into everyday social relations' (p. 119). The study shows how in the process of consumption the Walkman time and again broke the conventional boundaries, penetrating those liminal spaces beyond the pale, invalidating several theories to earn the categorisation of 'ambivalent' and 'hybrid' increasingly used in postmodern discourse. Yet it is significant that the conclusion finds it necessary to inform the reader how Sony developed the product in response to consumer demand and market research in order to be able to close the loop between production and consumption 'lest the idea of the "circuit"be receding from your grasp', ending with the reminder 'Remember the circuit now?', as if anyone reading the series could possibly forget.

Popular Culture and Everyday Life is also a textbook, claiming in all of 224 pages to offer a survey of social and cultural theory as well as a new approach to the study of popular culture. Written by two American media studies academics, it suggests that the study of popular culture should start from its embeddedness in ordinary everyday practices, presenting four case studies: food/eating, sport, selfhelp/therapy, and talking. It acknowledges the need to take historical specificity into account and proposes the use of ethnographic methods of investigation in conjunction with a selection of theoretical tools derived from discourse theory, anthropology, economics and media studies which it calls 'ethnomethodologically inspired cultural studies', or 'EMICS'. It takes a critical stance towards the 'speculative' nature of recent writings on popular culture, in similar vein to Angela McRobbie's critique of current trends in cultural studies in Back to Reality,4⁵ bringing back a welcome credibility to empirical 'data-driven' research into the particularity of the overlooked world of the everyday. Popular Culture and Everyday Life favours the 'mundane' over the 'spectacular' arguing that there has been too much emphasis placed on subcultures as knowing resistance to the ideological pull of dominant culture, questioning 'its claims to empower'. The critical acknowledgement of 'the distance between the site-specific interest associated with ethnomethodology and cultural studies' claim to understand entire ways of life' is particularly well exemplified by the 'circuit of culture' model. In the conclusion Miller and McHoul, referring to 'cultural devices', assert that 'the crucial question is: how do these figure in actual historical arrangements and in everyday events?', emphasizing the 'particularities and specificities of each case and how they might or might not be connected with wider issues'.

The clear style gives a deceptively readable historiography and development of cultural studies of 'the popular' and 'the everyday' combined with practical case studies to illustrate 'how to' conduct research in what the authors claim to be a 'new'way. But rather than a refutation they present their methodology as an intervention into the current state of cultural studies. The textbook format with bite-size references to a wide range of theoretical concepts does not give enough context and may be too concise for students uninitiated into the subject. Nevertheless, the extensive bibliography offers useful additional material. The somewhat eccentric glossary, however, displays a less successful attempt to systematise what is a very eclectic interdisciplinary project. Its virtue is that it allows room to investigate 'everyday' practices without a proforma. The missing ingredient, though not necessarily surprising in view of the two authors'media studies background, is the material world of goods.

The wealth of literature relating to material culture studies signals how much interest and relevance this field holds for academics in many disciplines at the present time. It would therefore seem to offer an excellent opportunity for design historians to engage much more actively in some of the lively debates that are flourishing beyond the boundaries that at times tends to confine rather than define our discipline.
