                                Ultimately-to use an intellectually compromised and politically
                                discredited word-we  are all probably investigating what (in
                           Adrian Forty'swords) "we are able to say about the actions of indi-

                       much to the disappointment of unreformedModernists and some
                               curatorsof white cubic museum. It exists, instead, in real lives, real
                                   situations,real places, and real time.
                                  Modernistdesigners,of course,believed that they were treat-
                               ing design as a means to an end of a social project. The most
                           committedof Modernists,Laszlo Moholy-Nagy,declared:"Not the
                              product,but man, is the end in view."But the Modernistconception
                                 of "man"(let alone woman) left much to be desired because men
                          and women inevitablyfailed to live up to the Platonicperfectionof
                              phileban solids and beautiful forms. It was Reyner Banham who
                              pointed out that the proof of the pudding is in the eating, not in the
                              design of the dish.
                               Banham'sConfucian-likewisdom was uttered in the 1960s;
                          and his influence was felt profoundly,if sometimes grudgingly,by
                               the firstgenerationof design historiansin the 1970s.VictorMargolin
                                         is right in his dating of the shift from a Pevsnerian, Modernist
                           approachto design history,to a Banhamesqueone aroundthis time.
                         The design criticismthat Banhamwas publishing in New Statesman
                          and New Society,beginning in the 1950s, opened up the study of
                              design history to include almost anything from the design and
                              graphicsof cigarettepackaging,throughtelevision puppet series, to
                            Argentinean decorated buses. This shift was not the result of
                          Banham'swriting; rather,the writing was, itself, a symptom of a
                          paradigm shift resulting from the explosion of an urban, sophisti-
                              cated popular culture. Design objects (and graphics, too) were no

                  The task of establishing the boundaries of design history
              would be a futile one, for the very reasons that Margolinoutlines.
               Trying to package areas of investigation into discreet and self-
                contained entities is condemned to failure. The ontological argu-
               ments in the first half of this century about what constituted "art"
              were historically necessary and, indeed, valuable as a way of
               combating prejudicesand liberating possibilities. To continue the
               arguments into the next century, however, whether by means of
                  textual or visual discourses, seems to me to be a barrenand irrele-
                vant activity. The question "Whatis art?"is now inert, irrelevant
              and boring because other concerns are now far more alive and
                 urgent.So, too, is the question "Whatis design history?"Thereis no
                neat answer,because of the growth and diversity of differenttypes
                  of design research. Design history could attempt to define itself
               narrowlyand exclusively,but this is so fraughtwith difficultiesand
                 contradictionsthat such an attempt is likely to be self-defeating.
                Design history societies would have to admit only those who
                pledged to researchand write in as particularand conventionally
                defined way. A graduate seeking membership would quake with
                 fear when asked "Areyou, or have you ever been, a member of a

Design history in the sense of historical detective work
                             concerningdesign is, of course, a crucialcomponent of writing and
                              thinking about design, and it is the lifeblood of those who use such
                             information and researchas a means to their own end. However,
                                   this type of researchis but one componentof what goes on in study-
                               ing design, and to claim that all that is going on now could use the
                            umbrella term 'design history" is not tenable. "Design studies" is
                          an infinitely more appropriate term for the plural histories and
                          myriad activities, approaches and methodologies that involve
                        human-made products and images. "Design studies" far better
                                   reflects the different ways of looking employed by the variety of
                                individuals and groups (whether defined in gender or race, or
                            whatever ternis), in their attempts to relate objects and images to
                          power and politics.
                                    History may not be the dominant organizing method in
                               these studies, which may well be synchronicratherthan diachronic.
                          But that is not to say that an historicalperspective is of little value.
                                   I can thinkof no culturalor design study from which historyshould
                           be absent for very long. Without an historical perspective, we are
                            unable to make judgmentsof worth, value, or significance;because
                       we are not seeing the presentas part of an historicalcontextor tradi-
                                    tion. Values change and are changed by historical circumstances,
                         and we need to be aware of those circumstanceswhen we pursue
                           our design studies.
                                   In the current debate about design studies versus design
                                  history, it is important to recognize the nature of both design and
                                  history. History should not be thought of as a fixed, ideal, tightly
                            contoured and comprehensivelydefined map of some supposedly
                                self-containedand well-guardedland. As is typical of virtuallyany
                           academicarea,design history'sdevelopmenthas resultedfrom indi-

culturalstudies (or philosophy)are frequentlyvaluableand relevant
                 (Baudrillardand Heidegger,even), but culturalstudies often suffers
              from a density and abstrusenessin both primaryand, more unfor-
                  givably, secondary and interpretativematerial.This should not be
                accepted meekly by those of us involved with design and educa-
                   tion. Complex ideas can be communicatedclearlywithout compro-
                mising them. Conventional design history, to its eternal credit,
                 usually was written in direct, simple and intelligible language.
                Culturalstudies writing also suffers too often from visual insensi-
                     tivity,and reveals an inabilityto make fine visual discriminations-
               perhaps not surprisingly when most of its exponents have had no
                  visual education. So I would say take on the ideas from cultural
                   studies, because they open up greaterpossibilities for understand-
                 ing design; but try to maintain design history's clarity and direct-
                 ness of communication,and visual sophistication.
                          I am not suggesting for a moment that all the ideas derived
              from cultural studies are constructive and liberating.Along with
               Adrian Forty,I would argue against that abuse of post-structuralist
                 thinking that decrees all values to be arbitraryand meaningless. A
                 dismissal of the facile "deathof values" thesis, and an engagement
                with values is, I think, one of the most pressing concerns for those
                  of us studying design in particular,and culturein general.
                      In spite of these reservations, the contribution of cultural
                  studies to the opening up of design history has been beneficial,and
                has changed design history to the point at which we should recog-
                  nize it as design studies. To apply a grammaticaland linguisticanal-
               ogy to design history, we could say that the first generation of
                 design historiansdeveloped design history from an obsession with
               nouns (discreet objects) to an emphasis on verbs (objects in use).
                Cultural studies has shown us that we must take into account not
                 only adjectives(the particularqualitiesand attributesof objects),but
                adverbs(the particularways in which an objectis being used). More
                  recently,post-structuralismhas taught us to question not only the
                    stability of the meanings of words, but even sentence construction
                and, most importantly,who is uttering the sentence to whom and
                  for what purpose.This has profoundimplicationsfor how we study
                  design, and has moved us far away from any presumed disinter-
                  ested and isolated study of objectsto an examinationof objects as
                 part of their wider social and cultural context. We have moved so
                  far from understandingdesign as a noun, that the replacementterm
                "design studies" which, for me, still has connotations of an over-
                concern with the object, seems both inevitable and necessary.
                "Design studies" isn't perfect,but it's closer to the mark in its rela-
                  tion to cultural studies. And, anyway, "perfect" is almost as
                unhealthya word as "ultimately!"
