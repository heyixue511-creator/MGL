   There was no such comparable institutional base in the USA. The
dominance on one side of art history, the museums, the College Art
Association, on the other of vocationally-minded design education
and of professional practices in design almost willfully blind to reflec-
tion, made the institutional setting for the development of design
studies incomparably more difficult.
   Yet  Victor and  his co-editors managed the  feat  of launching
Design Issues. And they did so with the deliberate intent that  it not
be an exclusively academic journal of history (as the Journal of
Design History was from 1987) but one that was capable of reflecting
at once on history and on practice—and a journal which, in its design
and organization, was  itself an embodiment of a sense of design
possibility and generosity (above all, as numerous authors and read-
ers have  gratefully  felt,  in  its boldness  of layout, with footnotes
accompanying the text in a side bar, an innovation that almost no
other academic  journals have had the  wit  to copy: the  journal
remains by  far the best-designed of those  in design history and
design studies).
      I stress the development of Design Issues (and the books which
Victor edited or co-edited which arose out of papers published in the
journal —Design Discourse  (1989); The  Idea  of Design  (1995);
Discovering Design (1995); The Designed World (2010))—as a major
virtue of all that he achieved, not only because the work and time
involved in such projects is enormous (and which can only be fully
known by those who have done such work) but because that com-
mitment, now 35 years in extent, is the mark of the wider generosity
and enthusiasm and care for both design and the possibility of creat-
ing a better world that characterized his life and work.
   Carried over into his speaking and writing (manifest in the essays
collected in the Politics of the Artificial (2002)) and above all, in per-
sonal terms, in the interactions with designers and scholars in the
field (and which included from the beginning his immediate and
enthusiastic support for the internationalization of the field through
ICDHS, the International Committee on Design History and Design
Studies) these concerns continued through to the last and in some
ways most extraordinary  gift he attempted to  offer the  field, his
World  History  of  Design  (2015,  with  volume  three    still  to
be published).

ing on the third aspect that emerged so strongly at his funeral, his
sense of humor. As a student at Columbia he contributed to MAD
magazine, edited the university humor magazine, the Jester and

                           As with his generosity, his humor was not outside his scholarship,
                                                             if more subtly embedded. Humor gets short-shrift  in academic—
                           even though the text that every philosopher of aesthetics would like
                                to see miraculously recovered is Artistotle’s reputed book on com-
                              edy. But more pertinent might be the comments that Richard Ellman
                               gives in his book Ulysses on the Liffey (1972) on Joyce’s decision
                                 that comedy and not tragedy should be the basis of Ulysses:— ‘To
                             Joyce, tragedy centered in privation, comedy in possession; tragedy
                                      in lamentation, comedy in joy; tragedy in elitism, comedy in democ-
                                racy. He liked comedy both  in  its immediate sense of provoking
                               laughter and in  its larger sense of negotiating the reconciliation of
                                forces. Sympathy and incongruity were his gregarious substitutes for
                                    pity and terror.’
                                      Victor, surely would have agreed—that the virtues at once of
