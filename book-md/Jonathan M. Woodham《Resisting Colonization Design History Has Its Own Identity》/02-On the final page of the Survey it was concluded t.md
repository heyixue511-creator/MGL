Although each of the [seven] courses has a distinct identity, they all share at least two common concerns. In Britain today, the number of freestanding, distinct design history degrees has expanded considerably, including courses at the Universities of Teesside, Lancashire and Derby, the London Institute (Camberwell), and the West Surrey College of Art and Design. Many others include design historical studies within a wider modular framework. For many years also in Britain there have been a small number of Masters programs in the field, the most noteworthy being those run at Middlesex University and at the Victoria and Albert Museum in conjunction with the Royal College of Art; both have very distinct identities within the spectrum of design historical studies in Britain.23 While Margolin may be right in stating that "there are no fomal doctoral programs in design history anywhere in the world, although there are a number of individuals who have received doctorates in Britain and the United States,"²4 he fails to acknowledge the general pattern of doctoral research in the arts and humanities in Britain. There are, in fact, established links between M.A. courses taught in design history and Ph.D. output, a trend which is likely to gather speed over the next few years.25 There also are other ways in which design historical research work may be consolidated within institutional frameworks, although these have not reached the levels of maturity or funding that may be found in other longstanding fields of historical research. For example, a Research Assistantship program "with a distinct identity within academia" resulting in a number of doctoral awards²⁶ has been in place since the early 1970s at the University of Brighton. A number of other British universities and institutions of higher education have also been active in this field. Whether the relative design historical research throughput at the doctoral level in Britain is as significant as might be desired in an academic discipline still in the process of consolidation at the highest academic levels is another matter. It should be noted that, historically, this has been due as much to the relative differentials of British government funding for research at the “old" and "new" universities (where design historical work almost exclusively resides); a very real problem which only has been addressed since the centralization of funding for the expanded university sector in Britain in the early 1990s.27

## Design History: A Discourse

Margolin expresses concern about what he perceives as the lack of "a recognizable body of knowledge which can be unequivocally labeled 'design history,"" a phrase coined by John E. Blake of the British Design Council at the 1977 National Design History Conference held in Brighton, England.28 This was an important conference, because there were many participants from different backgrounds from which they had moved into design history. As Penny Sparke, the editor of the collected papers, stated:

The subject matter of the conference was design history, itself, and the approach was a pluralistic one, demonstrating that there, are in fact, many design histories—ones that emphasize links with the history of technology, others more strongly allied to social history and technology, others more strongly allied to social history and sociology, and others that use terminology borrowed from the history of the fine and decorative arts. The interdisciplinary nature of the subject was reflected in the range of lectures, which were in three main sections that focused in turn on the designer, the consumer and the object. They covered, among others, such varied topics as railway design, relations between the designer and the law, wallpaper design, early American industrial design, and the professional structures available to graphic designers. These topics instantly laid bare the bones of such burning aesthetic and socio-cultural issues as taste, style, individualism versus anonymity in design, the social and economic restraints on the design process, the relevance of quality judgments in design history and, more fundamentally, what is design history and for whom is the discipline being developed.29

It is difficult to see why Margolin still continues to attach such importance to the methodological limitations of Pevsner's Pioneers of the Modern Movement of 1936 (later revised as Pioneers of Modern Design) at a time when the methodological possibilities of design history have moved on considerably.³º What is presented as "Pevsner's restrictive view of objects worthy of historical investigation" is no more questionable than the focus of the design collecting policy of MOMA in New York, the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, or the CCI in Paris,³1 indeed, the absence of any museological analysis as a conditioning factor in the nature and substance of design history studies, let alone its potency in shaping public (mis)conceptions, seems somewhat myopic.³2 But to return to Pevsner, it is not as though his thesis has not been addressed elsewhere, perhaps most obviously by Herwin Schaefer in his 1970 text, The Roots of Modern Design: the Functional Tradition in the 19th Century³3 Schaefer, who was acknowledged by Pevsner to have been of great assistance in the preparation of the 1949 Museum of Modern Art edition of Pioneers, was critical of Pevsner's undue emphasis on "the artistic creativity of individuals," and dwelt instead on the history of functionalism in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, concentrating on those strands of machine and anonymous design which he felt were lacking in Pevsner's work. Pevsner's portrayal of the Victorian period as one typified by the frighteningly ugly designs enjoyed by a complacent and philistine bourgeoisie and which was to be rescued from an aesthetic low tide by the pioneering efforts of Pugin, Morris and Ruskin, was given relatively short shrift.

Another well-known alternative to the Pevsnerian perspective is to be found in Siegfried Giedion's Mechanization Takes Command, published in 1948 with the subtitle “a contribution to anonymous history."³4Giedion stressed the need for research into "the links between industrial methods and methods used outside industry—in art, in visualization." He also called for Chairs of Anonymous History to be set up in universities in order to examine, and establish, the ways in which facts and figures might be gathered; also, the means by which their relationship to society, in terms of cultural impact and meaning, might be assessed.

Much more recently, Matthew Turner, in an attack on what he characterized as the narrow geopolitical and aesthetic base on which design history was established, proposed the exploration of registered designs as another possible means of escaping from the tyranny of “an extremely limited range of Western cult objects." He stated that:

Designs of an altogether humbler nature occupy the bulk of the registers, and accordingly represent the manmade world as it is encountered by most of us, most of the time. Overlooked, if not despised by mainstream design historians, this is the world of vegetable peelers (our kitchen possesses one with U.S. Des. 2232940), candy dispensers, coffin handles, and ballpoint pens. It can range from 19th century Indian bullock carts to 20th century atomic radiation shields; from badges with the legend "Remember Pearl Harbor"(1942) to artificial limbs.

Those who cleave to Modern Design principles may regret that so many lampshades and Christmas trees have survived in graphic detail, while addicts might be delighted to reconstruct that missing piece of 1930s tableware. But for the historian of design the registers, representations and statistics of Design will provide valuable additional source material, and may suggest new lines of enquiry.35

Philip Pacey proposed another mode of action, contending that design history,

by acknowledging the truth of the concept of everyone as a designer without dismissing it as a 'truism' and by complementing the study of professional design with recognition of the prior and current activities of non-professional designers, can encourage the design profession radically to redefine its role vis-a-vis people at large, for the enrichment of all concerned. At the same time design history will find a way of climbing out of a trap which it surely did not intend to set for itself, that of seeming exclusively concerned with an activity associated with the industrialized, affluent West.36

Historiography notwithstanding it is interesting to note that Margolin appears to have overlooked the significance of Adrian Forty's book, Objects of Desire: Design and Society.37 It should be remembered that the manuscript was completed in 1980 and, although not opening up a number of the key concerns articulated later by Buckley, Attfield, Kirkham and other feminist writers of design history admired by Margolin, the text is still an important marker in the demotion of the centrality of the designer in design historical accounts. Its reception in the design press was understandably cool as its readers, buoyed up by the "Designer hype" of the 1980s, saw the notion of the "individual creative genius" seriously undermined. Forty suggested that because,

designers generally talk and write only about what they do themselves, design has come to be regarded as belonging entirely within their realm. This misunderstanding has been repeated in innumerable books and in the coverage of design in the press and television. It has also, with more serious consequences, been taught in schools of design, where students are liable to acquire grandiose illusions about the nature of their skills, with the result that they encounter all manner of difficulties in their subsequent careers.38

One reviewer in North America saw Objects of Desire as “a hatchet job on designers and design history alike"³9 before going on to ask:

What is meant, ask Forty and his friends, by the history of design? It cannot be merely the changing sequence of admired or characteristic shapes, profiles, and materials over the decades and centuries, as museums and exhibitions of the applied arts tend to suggest. Nor can it be just an account of the talents and methods of the Raymond Loewys, Gordon Russells and Ettore Sottsasses of this world. Investigating the genesis of almost any object of industrial manufacture, striking or banal, one finds a maze of processes, influences, and ideas which often leave the designer (if indeed such a person can be identified) subordinate and peripheral, if not downright irrelevant, to an understanding of the object's value and significance.“0

That it was an attack on what Forty took to be prevalent design historical practice is unquestionable,41 although the territory that he covered was relatively familiar to those involved in teaching specialist design history on freestanding degree and postgraduate courses in Britain in the early 1980s. It naturally became another ingredient of considerations about the potential range and scope of design historical practice, an academic vista which changed signally as a deeper knowledge of more than the visible tip of the design historical iceberg, epitomized by exhibitions, museum displays and publications which too often reinforced the stereotypes attacked by Dilnot, Buckley and others, reached the wider public domain.

That design history is a field potentially involving analysis of modern consumption was recognized by anthropologists (as well as design historians), even if, in 1987, the discipline could be labeled "bizarre" by Daniel Miller, an anthropologist from University College, London. Like Forty, he attacked what he believed to be its conventional mode of study which was, he claimed, "a form of pseudo art history, in which the task is to locate great individuals such as Raymond Loewy or Norman Bel Geddes and portray them as the creators of modern mass culture."42 Nonetheless he also acknowledges Forty as providing “an effective critique" of such a view, before going on to say that:

Forty provides abundant examples of the complex manner in which commerce developed new goods around perceived divisions in the target population and a series of beliefs about the nature of hygiene, domesticity, science and modernity which became enshrined in and reproduced through the appearance of everyday objects, although again the transformation of goods in consumption is largely ignored.⁴3

Miller's work, together with that of other writers on consumption,“ is now well known to many design historians, particularly as studies in material culture increasingly are being explored. British design historians also have contributed to Winterthur conferences in the United States which deal with many aspects of material culture, as indeed does the Winterthur Portfolio,4⁵ which contains much material of value. Miller's positive influence on design historical debate stemmed from his contribution as a keynote speaker at the International Design History Society Conference mounted in 1987 at the embryonic Design Museum, London, and Brighton Polytechnic.4 As Christopher Bailey remarked at the time,

The first was that students of design history, working perhaps on the products of the electrical goods industry, might lack the materials for understanding the ways in which new products were received by consumers.
