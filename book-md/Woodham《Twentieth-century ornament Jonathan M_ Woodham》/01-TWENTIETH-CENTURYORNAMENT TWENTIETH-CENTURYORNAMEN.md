# TWENTIETH-CENTURY ORNAMENT

# JONATHAN M. WOODHAM

During the twentieth century ornament has been pressed into an unprecedentedly wide range of applications in every field—including architecture, furniture, products, textiles and ceramics— and at every level, from the élite to the vernacular. The result has been an extraordinary crossfertilization of ideas and sources, in which radical design movements have competed and conflicted with ingrained traditions of ornament which provide a sense of security and stability.

In this beautiful and fascinating book, Jonathan Woodham traces these developments in detail, decade by decade, showing that for every influence from fine art there was a countervailing popular tradition, often of nostalgia, sometimes of conscious nationalism. He puts the analysis of ornament into its proper context of everyday living and use, covering everything from surface pattern application in ceramics and textiles to three-dimensional ornament on buildings, automobiles and household goods. A passion for the oriental and exotic, for example, evidenced in the hugely popular theme' cinemas of the 1930s, found its way on to perfume labels and chocolate wrappers. The chrome detailing on the dream cars’ of 1950s America was repeated on items as disparate as refrigerators and jukeboxes.

The work of major and influential designers is fully discussed, but so also is the anonymous vernacular—kitchen equipment, popular furnishings and tableware. In particular Jonathan Woodham gives proper stress to the enormously popular exhibitions such as the Century of Progress in Chicago of 1933 and 1934, and the Festival of Britain of 1951, which were attended by millions of people. They had an incalculable influence on the attitude towards design and ornament among ordinary purchasers of goods.

Wide-ranging in its scope, lavishly illustrated and produced, Twentieth-Century Ornament is a classic source book, which will provide essential reference for designers, students, social historians and collectors, as well as offering an absorbing portrait of the century for the general reader.

With 400 illustrations, 200 in colour
